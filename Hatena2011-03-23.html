At 210 Bq per kg of water, the effective dose from an oral intake of 10,000 Bq is 0.22 mSv, and at 100 mSv, the decision is made to err on the safe side (based on data from a one-year-old child, the most sensitive child) and "give him a stabilizing iodine pill". So, let's see, drinking 21.6 tons of water would be the level of stable iodine needed, which can be achieved in a year if you drink 59 liters a day. Oh, but is 100 mSv an index for intake by breathing? Where is the value for drinking as water written? Hmmm. I wonder if the difference between drinking and inhaling can be absorbed there, since the effective dose calculation table for converting becquerels to sieverts includes the cases of inhaling and drinking. According to the <a href='http://researchcompliance.uc.edu/radsafety/isotope/isds-I131.html'>second opinion</a>, this calculation seems to tip it about 1.5 times to the safe side. Using these figures, you would need to drink about 90 liters a day.

If we use the phrase "The probability of thyroid cancer after thyroid exposure of 1 Gy or less is extremely low for people 20 years of age or older", it would be 1000 mSv, which would be "The probability of thyroid cancer is extremely low at an intake of 216 tons or less". If this water is not drunk for 50 years at 12 liters per day, the probability of thyroid cancer is "extremely low. No matter how you calculate it, there is no reason for people without children to buy up all the water, and old men and women over 20 should give it to their children.

I also wondered what about women who might have children in the future, but with a half-life of 8 days, that means it decreases to 3 parts per trillion in one year, so I guess I shouldn't worry about that.
