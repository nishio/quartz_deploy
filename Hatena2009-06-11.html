Now, since I suspect that many readers of this blog are information technology people, let me explain the background of this piece in that context.
<strong>I hate unrepeatable bugs</strong>. <strong>It's not nice when 100 / 10 is "roughly 10, sometimes 11". <strong>We don't like it when things that should match don't match exactly</strong>, and we don't want y + x to be 9.9609375 when x + y is 10. <strong>We don't want people to repeat themselves</strong>. principle, right? Computers are better at repetitive tasks, so we want to leave it to them and concentrate on higher-level instructions on what to repeat.


Programmers have their own "aesthetics" and create "beautiful" code according to their own aesthetics. However, this "beauty" is not well understood by non-programmers. That's right. We express ourselves in code, a medium that only we can understand. If you were a creature that could see ultraviolet light, and you drew a beautiful picture using ultraviolet light, most of mankind would not understand it. If you think it is beautiful, you should not only say, "This is beautiful," but also bring it into the visible light range that we can see, even if it means sacrificing a little expression. I think the reverse is also true. I think that the rigor, symmetry, and regularity that I love and find "beautiful" should be expressed in the form of, for example, a two-dimensional physical object that can be easily understood by non-programmers.


Of course, at this point in time, I do not believe that my work is yet complete. But if the "design guidelines" I wrote above caught your attention and made you want me to create more with these design guidelines, I would be very grateful if you could <a href='http://www.tagboat.com/ngs/award/summer/2009/initial_screening. html'>poll page</a>, search for Virtual Star and press the vote button. It will be very encouraging.

The name "Virtual Star" comes from the fact that although stars are visible to the human eye, they are actually represented only by circles. It looks like it is full of straight lines, but in reality, it is composed entirely of circles. The golden ratio is the ratio of the diameter of the parent circle to the distance of the child circle, and the ratio of the diameter of the parent circle to the diameter of the child circle. When the golden ratio is used, a straight line like this is produced. Conversely, if the golden ratio is not used, the upper right circle of the lower left circle and the upper left circle of the lower right circle will not overlap, resulting in a large number of smaller circles and a black line. By choosing the appropriate ratio, we have the phenomenon that the grandchildren's circles overlap exactly.
