The author of this book is a translator of the Da Vinci Code and other books. He has been teaching English for more than a decade and seems to have collected patterns that everyone picks up on, so the book is pretty dense. Also, natural language is highly redundant, so there are many cases where a rough understanding is fine. That's why you don't notice when the foundation is being eaten away by termites. In a book like this, you might think, "Oh, this is how the English sentence is written, isn't it? It is difficult to realize it on your own unless you are told, "No, that is a common mistake.

>>
I bought the book, which I have not read yet.
<<

I made this mistake. It's such a seemingly simple sentence. So that's how much of the basics are missing. I said, "I bought the book. I bought the book, which I have not read yet." I translated it as "I bought the book, which I have not read yet. Wrong.

>>
I bought the book. I have not read the book yet.
<<

The important point is that the first half of the verb is in the past tense while the second half is in the present perfect tense. The important point is that the first half of the verb is in the past tense, but the second half is in the present perfect tense. Also, the reason why it becomes "I bought the book" instead of "I bought the book" is because it is the book, not a book.

One more thing.

>>
You should not buy new clothes because they are cheap.
<<

The literal translation would be "you shouldn't buy new clothes, because they are cheap", but why shouldn't you buy them if they are cheap? So I translated it as "you shouldn't buy new clothes, because they are cheap", but that wasn't the point.

This omits just and only from the original sentence.

>>
You should not buy new clothes just because they are cheap.
<<

As far as English is concerned, a sentence is easier to understand if "just" is not omitted. However, as a practical matter, it is not true that all English authors in the world write easy-to-understand sentences. It is obvious if you think about whether Japanese people write easy-to-understand Japanese or not.

There are a lot of problems like this, where the words themselves are simple but difficult to translate, or where the translator thinks he or she can translate the words but is mistaken.
