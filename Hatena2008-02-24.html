*1203858255* Notes on presenting the same content.
What we tend to do when presenting the same content more than once (especially when there are people in the audience who have seen the same content presented before).

- "This is the material I presented at ________."
- "I know some of you have seen it."
- Sorry for the repetition.
- Not much has changed, though.
- I'll skip this one because I've done it before.
- "It's a thing because it's spoiled (to the point of laughter)."

Since the target audience for that presentation is those who have not yet heard it, we should not spoil the experience for first-time listeners by worrying too much about what those who have already heard the presentation think. For first-time listeners, statements like the above are "useless information" or "offensive" or "alienating".

This is not just about today's announcement by id:s******; I have had a vague, hazy feeling ever since I saw id:s******_***'s announcement in December and id:i**_***'s announcement the other day.

Extra. When you introduce yourself in a place where people already know you, the embarrassment of introducing yourself causes you to lie about your identity in order to get a laugh.
- "I've been learning AS3 lately, and I write a little bit of Python, maybe one line."
- "I'm from java-ja. I've been studying AS3 lately."
Since the target audience for self-introductions is people who do not know you, you should not try to gain popularity by introducing yourself in this way. The more you make your story popular, the more the people to whom you should have introduced yourself will feel alienated, wondering why they are the only ones laughing around you.
