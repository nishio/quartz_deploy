*1214132228* [book review] My First Ruby
Yugui gave me <a href="http://www.amazon.co.jp/dp/4873113679/pages20-22">my first Ruby</a>.
The term "Ruby for Beginners" tends to be ambiguous, blurring whether it is for "people who are new to programming" or "people who are already experienced in programming but not yet experienced in Ruby," but this book clearly states that it is the latter. I see, so the target audience is people like me who are already using Python. This is a good thing I got.


It is amazing that every chapter has a "summary of this chapter" and a "what this chapter does not cover" section. Also, the differences between Ruby 1.8 and 1.9 are well written. I didn't understand the difference between 1.8 and 1.9 until now, but in short, Ruby 2.9 is the equivalent of Python 3000, which is "drastically changed to make it better! Ruby1.9 is a stepping stone toward Ruby2.0, which is equivalent to Python3000.


I also find the enclosed columns and one point about trap marks very interesting. They list various things that you might get stuck on when you move to Ruby from another language. For example, the phenomenon below, which was recently discussed in 86 chat.

>||
irb(main):007:0> (10 - 1
irb(main):008:1> - 2 - 3)
=> -5
||<

The newline character is recognized as the end of a minute, as is the semicolon. For this reason, it cannot be inserted anywhere you like, like a space or a tab." So I hear.

By the way, if you do the same thing in Python, you get this.
>||
>>> (10 - 1
    - 2 - 3)
4
||<

This is what Ruby's behavior is all about, isn't it? If you understand it, it is a reasonable behavior.
>||
irb(main):010:0> (10 - 1 - 2 - 3)
=> 4
irb(main):011:0> (10 - 1; - 2 - 3)
=> -5
||<

Off topic. When people who are used to other languages learn a new language, they tend to make assumptions about how it will work. It may be important to look at examples of such pitfalls before getting stuck.
